# CS-230-Operating-Platforms
# Briefly summarize The Gaming Room client and their software requirements. Who was the client? What type of software did they want you to design?
So, the project was with The Gaming Room, which initially came up with the game Draw It or Lose It just for Android. They were looking to take it to the next level by creating a web-based version that could be played on desktops and mobile devices. What they needed was a multiplayer game system that worked in browsers, allowing different teams and players to join in, all while keeping things under control with rules like unique usernames and a single game instance for everyone. Basically, they wanted to make the game more accessible and scalable, all while ensuring that the gameplay felt the same for everyone.
# What did you do particularly well in developing this documentation?
One thing I feel I nailed while working on this documentation was how I organized the system requirements and tied them back to the design decisions. I made it a point to explain not just what the system needs to do, but also the reasons behind certain design choices, like opting for a singleton pattern or a client-server architecture. I also think I did a solid job of expanding on each section and providing enough detail, showing that I really grasp how the system operates as a whole instead of just throwing out the basics.
# What about the process of working through a design document did you find helpful when developing the code?
Working through the design document really helped when I was coding. It made me think about how everything should be structured before diving into the actual writing. Planning out the classes, their relationships, and the overall system behavior ahead of time made the whole coding process way smoother and kept confusion to a minimum. Instead of just guessing how different parts would connect, I had a solid blueprint from the UML diagram and the design write-up. It also helped me spot some potential issues early on, so I wasn’t dealing with mistakes during the implementation stage.
# If you could choose one part of your work on these documents to revise, what would you pick? How would you improve it?
If I could tweak one part of my work, it would definitely be the evaluation section where I compared the different platforms. I mentioned the pros and cons of each, but I think I could really strengthen the connections to what the client specifically needs. Instead of just talking about the platforms in a general way, I could make it more relatable by directly linking their strengths and weaknesses to what’s needed for a multiplayer web-based game. That way, the evaluation would feel more on point and relevant to the project.
# How did you interpret the user’s needs and implement them into your software design? Why is it so important to consider the user’s needs when designing?
When I was figuring out what the user really needed, I made sure to focus on the important stuff the client laid out and turned that into features in the design. For instance, since they wanted unique names, I added some validation logic and a centralized storage system. They also needed just one game instance, so I opted for the singleton pattern. I tried to visualize how each of these requirements would shape the system and made it a point to reflect those needs in the design. It's super important to keep the user's needs in mind because, at the end of the day, the software is there to solve their problems. If the design doesn’t align with what they actually want, even the best-built system can fall short and make things a hassle to use.
# How did you approach designing software? What techniques or strategies would you use in the future to analyze and design a similar software application?
When it came to the software design, I kicked things off by breaking the system into smaller pieces and identifying the main objects like games, teams, and players. Then, I thought about how these objects would interact with each other and used some object-oriented concepts like inheritance and encapsulation to organize them better. I also considered the overall structure, going with a client-server model because it made the most sense for a web-based multiplayer game. Moving forward, I plan to stick with similar strategies—starting with the requirements, sketching out diagrams, and planning the system structure before diving into coding. I’d also want to pay more attention to scalability and real-life usage scenarios, like managing a ton of users or handling network hiccups, to really strengthen the design.
